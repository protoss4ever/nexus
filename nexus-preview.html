Recommend
How AI finds connections across things that seem unrelated on the surface.

The system isn't matching by category or label — it's measuring latent similarity across many dimensions at once. "Early mornings" and "crime novels" don't look alike, but they share qualities like slowness, sustained attention, and solitude. That's why a recommendation can feel both surprising and right.

A harder question.

What counts as similar? Two people who both enjoy hiking might have nothing else in common — one craves solitude, the other the social ritual. The system has to choose which dimensions to measure, and that choice shapes everything that gets recommended.

This is why recommendation systems can feel uncanny — and why they sometimes feel completely off. The dimensions being measured are often invisible to the user, and sometimes to the designers too.

Group
How AI finds structure in things that don't come with labels.

These are cities. What do you think they have in common?

The system grouped by patterns across climate, density, and geography simultaneously — not by country or continent. That's why Tokyo and São Paulo end up together (dense, humid, coastal-adjacent megacities), while Reykjavik lands with Zurich (small, cold-climate, high-livability). The groupings reflect structure the data contained, not labels anyone assigned.

The system doesn't know what "type" of city these are — it only sees numbers. Population density, average temperature, geographic latitude. It finds which cities are close to each other across all those dimensions at once. The labels come after the clusters are found, not before.

With fewer groups, the system is forced to merge cities that are somewhat different. With more, it splits things that were close but not identical. Neither answer is wrong — it depends on what question you're asking.

Ready to try this on your own data?

A harder question.

Who decides how many groups there should be? The algorithm doesn't. Ask for 2 and you get broad categories. Ask for 8 and you get fine-grained distinctions that may or may not be meaningful. The number of clusters is always a human decision — and it shapes everything the system finds.

This means two researchers using the same algorithm on the same data can reach completely different conclusions — not because one is wrong, but because they asked different questions before they started.

Predict
How AI extends patterns forward — and why it can be confidently wrong.
Weekly step counts — someone's last 10 weeks

Look at the shape before you click. Where do you think it goes?

The system averaged through the week-to-week variation to find the underlying trend. Individual dips — illness, travel, a bad week — get smoothed out. What remains is the general direction. This is useful, but it also means the system will miss a genuine change in behavior until enough new data overrides the old trend.

The system doesn't predict the exact next value — it finds the trend beneath the noise and projects it forward. That's why it can be simultaneously reasonable and wrong: the trend was real, but something changed. The further forward you predict, the wider the uncertainty grows.

The prediction is always a bet on the past continuing. It has no way to know when something fundamentally changes.

Ready to try this on your own data?

A harder question.

How far out can you trust a prediction? Uncertainty compounds. One step ahead is much more reliable than ten steps ahead. And if the underlying pattern changes — a product launch, a policy shift, an external event — the model has no way to know. It keeps predicting based on what it learned, even when the world has already shifted.

This is the central limitation of all prediction: the model is always reasoning about the future using evidence from the past. It cannot detect a regime change until after it has already happened and enough new data has accumulated to override the old pattern.
